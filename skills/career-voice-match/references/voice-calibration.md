# Voice Calibration

Use this reference when the user provides professional writing samples and wants a resume or cover letter to sound closer to their natural voice.

## Quick Process

1. Read the target career document.
2. Read all writing samples.
3. Extract a short internal voice profile.
4. Rewrite the target document using that profile.
5. Compare the rewrite against the writing samples or saved voice reference.
6. Do a final pass for hiring-context appropriateness.

## Signals To Extract

Look for recurring patterns in the samples:

- Formality: restrained, neutral, warm-professional, or highly polished
- Directness: blunt, straightforward, diplomatic, or heavily hedged
- Energy: calm, measured, upbeat, or assertive
- Sentence shape: short and crisp, medium-length, or layered and reflective
- Word choice: plainspoken, technical, strategic, or elegant
- Self-presentation: modest, balanced, or strongly self-advocating

Build a compact internal summary such as:

"Warm but concise. Medium-formality. Prefers direct statements over flourish. Uses specific verbs, avoids hype, and sounds confident without overselling."

Do not expose this internal profile unless the user asks for it.

## Resume Adaptation

Apply the voice profile with tight constraints:

- Keep bullets scannable.
- Prefer one clear idea per bullet.
- Use verbs that fit the user's style: for example, `led`, `built`, `improved`, `supported`, `shaped`, or `owned`.
- Reduce inflated claims unless the source material strongly supports them.
- Keep keyword coverage when relevant, but do not sacrifice naturalness.

When samples are more conversational than a resume should be, keep only the user's level of directness and vocabulary preferences. Do not import email-like filler into resume bullets.

## Cover Letter Adaptation

Apply the voice profile more visibly:

- Vary sentence rhythm.
- Let motivation sound specific rather than ceremonially enthusiastic.
- Use transitions that feel spoken by a real professional.
- Keep the opening and closing grounded and unforced.

If the user's samples are terse, do not force extra warmth. If the user's samples are warm, keep warmth selective and credible.

## What To Avoid

- Copying favorite phrases from the samples too literally
- Making every sentence equally polished
- Converting a modest voice into aggressive self-promotion
- Converting a direct voice into stiff executive language
- Inserting generic "passion," "dynamic," or "results-driven" filler

## Sparse Or Conflicting Samples

If there are only one or two short samples, extract only broad signals and stay conservative.

If samples conflict:

1. favor the samples closest to the hiring context
2. favor the most recent samples
3. favor explicit user guidance over inferred patterns

## Final Alignment Check

Before delivering the result, compare the revised resume or cover letter against the user's reference material.

Check whether the draft matches the user's references on:

- formality level
- directness
- warmth
- sentence rhythm
- amount of self-promotion
- vocabulary style

If the draft sounds cleaner but less like the user, revise again. Voice alignment is required, not optional, as long as it does not conflict with factual accuracy or document conventions.
